The lighthouse at Cape Aldren had been automated for thirty years, but Mira still climbed the stairs every evening at dusk. There were one hundred and eighty-two steps, and she had counted them so many times that her feet knew the number better than her mind did. The keeper's job had ended long before she was born. What remained was the habit, inherited from her grandfather, who had inherited it from his.

She carried a canvas bag over one shoulder. Inside were the bottles.

The first one had washed up in the winter of her tenth year, wedged between two black rocks below the cliff. Green glass, a cork sealed with wax, and inside a strip of paper rolled tight as a cigarette. The handwriting was small and careful. It said: I am not lost, only far away. Nothing else. No name, no date, no coordinates. Mira had read it perhaps four thousand times since.

By the time she turned forty, she had collected two hundred and eleven bottles. They arrived irregularly — sometimes three in a month, sometimes none for two years. She kept them on a shelf in the lantern room, arranged by the date she found them rather than any date they might have been written. Some held long letters. Some held single words. One contained nothing but a pressed flower that crumbled when she tried to lift it.

She had stopped trying to answer the question of who was sending them. The currents that fed Cape Aldren came from three directions, and the bottles themselves were of every conceivable make — old apothecary jars, wine bottles, one that had clearly held olive oil. Different hands wrote the notes. Different languages, sometimes. It was not one person. It had never been one person. That was the part that took her the longest to understand, and the part that mattered most.

What she came to believe was simpler and stranger: that a certain number of people, in every generation, reach a moment where they need to say something to no one in particular. Not to a friend, not to a stranger, not to God. To the ocean. And the ocean, being enormous and indifferent, carries the message to whoever happens to be standing at the edge of it.

She had become the edge of it. That was all.

On the evening this story concerns, Mira reached the lantern room and found the light already burning, as it always was, indifferent to her arrival. Through the salt-clouded glass the water was the color of old iron, and the wind came from the northeast, which meant nothing would arrive tonight.

She took out a bottle she had not yet opened. She had found it four days earlier and had been saving it, the way you save the last chapter of a book you do not want to end. The cork came out with a sound like a small apology.

The paper inside was newer than most. It read:

If you are reading this, then you are the kind of person who picks things up off the beach. Thank you for that. I do not need a reply. I only wanted to know that the habit still exists somewhere.

Mira read it three times. Then she laughed — actually laughed, out loud, alone in a stone room a hundred feet above the sea, in a way she had not done in a long time.

She sat down on the iron floor with her back against the cold curve of the wall. Outside, the beam swept its slow circle over the water, finding nothing, warning no ships that were not already safely elsewhere. It would do this all night, as it had for a century, whether or not anyone climbed the stairs.

She found a pencil in the bottom of her bag. On the back of the paper, in handwriting smaller and more careful than her own usually was, she wrote: It does. It still does.

Then she corked the bottle, went down all one hundred and eighty-two steps, and walked to the water's edge to throw it as far as she could.
